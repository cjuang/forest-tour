Fisher Meteorological Station

If you need real-time weather data, the Fisher Meteorological Station has been recording data since 2001. All data is available on the Harvard Forest website.

Stop 1

Raup House

The Raup House, also known as the Director's House, was designed by and named after Hugh M. Raup, Harvard Forest Director (1946-1967).

Stop 1

Fisher House

The Fisher House is named after Richard Thornton Fisher (1876-1934), the first director of Harvard Forest (1907-1934) and a professor at the Harvard Business School. The house was likely built around the time of Fisher's marriage with Georgina Paine in 1913. The houses at this stop are now dormitories for visiting students, scientists, and conference groups.

Stop 1

Stone Wall

These rocks didn't stack themselves! This is a piece of the original cropland border within John and Molly Sanderson's 50-acre farm (est. 1763). The original Sanderson farmhouse, now called Community House, still stands at Harvard Forest.

Stop 9

Snag

Snags are Ecologically Important

Although not alive, snags are the home or food source of many living creatures and organisms. How do different animals use snags? Click each box below to find out.

Raccoons and Squirrels

Raccoon families and squirrels can use hollow snags as tree dens. The loose bark and slits of a snag provide warmth and shelter during the winter.

Pileated Woodpeckers

Pileated woodpeckers excavate their own nest cavities in the trunk of snags by chiseling the bark with their strong neck muscles and beveled bills. They also use snags for food by pulling away at the bark to eat the insects underneath. Unoccupied woodpecker cavities can later become cavity nests for other birds or mammals who cannot make their own cavities.

Bald Eagles

Bald Eagles and other raptors like to use snags as perches to rest while hunting. They also an use the limbs or cavities of snags for nesting.

Wood Ducks

Wood ducks use snags for their nests near water. A day after wood ducklings hatch, the mother hen flies out of the cavity nest and calls out to her ducklings. The ducklings climb out of the cavity and leap from a height anywhere between 6 to 15 feet into the water below.


The More Snags, the Better

Snags more abundant in old growth structure. Old growth forests are forests that were never affected directly by humans whether through logging, agriculture, or other land uses. The forest canopies and floors are diverse in age and structure. As a result, they are important ecosystems and the habitat of many species of animals and plants, some of which can only survive in old growth conditions.

Compare this to second growth forests, which are forests that grew back after human land use. Clear-cutting or logging the area destroys the forest conditions that may have been adapted over thousands of years. The forest that grows back is no longer diverse and instead has even-aged, even-sized trees. Harvard Forest, like most New England Forests, are second growth forests because of NEw England colonization.

Stop 9

Private Hiking Trail

Most of New England's Forests are Privately-Owned

Our trail intersects with a trail owned by the Feldman family, who transformed their acres of land into miles of recreational trails and engaging in forest management strategies. The role of conserving and protecting the land extends beyond organizations like Harvard Forest.

To read more about the Feldmans, click here.


How Can You Preserve the Forest?

The easiest way to preserve the forest is to reduce your own environmental impact.

  • Minimize the amount of paper you print or receive, print double-sided, reuse paper, and recycle.
  • Eat more locally-grown foods to reduce clear-cutting of forests for agricultural lands.
  • Support your local land trust
  • Support forest conservation work and research

Did you know? In Massachusetts, you can purchase a Conservation Restriction (CR) Acquisition that can permanently protect a forest. Read more about CR and other ways to legally protect land for wildlife by clicking here.
